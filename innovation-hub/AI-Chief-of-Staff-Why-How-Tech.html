Why

An executive's attention is spent finding the work, not doing it.

A COO's morning is scattered across a calendar, two inboxes, an ERP approvals queue, a task tracker, and a dozen Slack threads. The signal is real — it's just buried. The job of a chief of staff is to collapse that noise into a single, ranked view of what needs a decision today.

How

A closed loop: sense the signal, reason against policy, then wait for the human.

The assistant is proactive but never autonomous on what matters. Every cycle runs the same six steps — and the fourth is a hard gate the executive owns.

01

Sense

Pull events, mail, approvals, tasks & updates from connected systems.

02

Reason

Apply preferences, client rules & approval policy to rank what matters.

03

Recommend

Draft the action — accept, reschedule, reply, approve, reassign.

04

Approve

The executive reviews, edits, overrides, defers or confirms. Human gate.

05

Act

Write the decision back to the source system & notify the right people.

06

Log

Record action, confidence & before/after for a complete audit trail.

Human-in-control is the product, not a setting. The AI may auto-approve only routine items that sit fully inside a configured policy. Anything high-value, off-policy, or externally-facing — sending email, approving over a limit, booking travel — is drafted and held for explicit confirmation.

Architecture

How data flows — from your systems, through reasoning, back under control.

Connectors read from the systems of record into a configurable context store. Intelligence engines reason over that context, every proposed action passes the human gate, and approved actions are written back to the source while a parallel audit stream captures everything.
